## What I did
I built a hand-set attention-style circuit that scores each item by its value-per-unit-weight (`v_i / w_i`) and discounts items that would consume a large fraction of the remaining capacity, encouraging smaller items when capacity is tight. The model function maps `(item_values, item_weights, capacity)` → `[batch, n_items]` scores, computes the circuit entirely on the GPU with PyTorch, and returns NumPy scores to `task.evaluate`. The evaluation runs over the full sweep of `n_items` (8, 10, 12), `tau` (0.3, 0.5, 0.7), and `rho` (-0.5, 0.0, 0.5), producing a canonical optimality ratio and three normalized lifts (over random, over greedy value, over greedy ratio).

## Why this visualisation
The app shows four numeric controls: the headline optimality (fraction of optimal value achieved at the canonical condition) and three relative lifts. Lifting the headline metric by a small absolute amount can be misleading — a 5% absolute improvement is far more significant if the baseline is 0.15 than if it is 0.85. Normalized lifts (`(model - baseline) / (optimal - baseline)`) compress the signal: they show how much of the gap between the baseline and the optimum the method closes. This makes it easy to see whether the hand-built policy substantially outperforms the "random" strawman, beats the "greedy value only" policy that ignores weight, and whether it can match or beat the strong fractional-optimal heuristic implemented by `greedy_by_ratio`.
