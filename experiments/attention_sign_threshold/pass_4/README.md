# What I did

This is a **trained** attempt (not hand-built like pass_3). The model is the smallest delta from `base_model.py`: a single attention head reduced to its QK pathway, scoring each pair with one **learned** bilinear form, `score = qᵀ M k`, with no value/output projection and no MLP. `M` (64×64) is discovered by gradient descent on a sign objective (attend iff `cos(q,k) > 0`, BCE loss) — nothing is hand-set. Training drives `M` toward a scaled identity (`cos(vec(M), vec(I)) ≈ 0.99`), i.e. the head *rediscovers the dot product* and a sharp threshold at cos = 0, beating the linear `max(0,cos)` baseline by a large lift. Because the circuit is learned, I add **causal evidence**: zeroing `M` (or keeping only its off-diagonal) collapses the sweep to a flat ~0.5, proving the learned diagonal circuit — not the metric — produces the sign flip.

# Why this visualisation

The Demo tab leads with the **ablation sweep**: mean attention vs cosine for the trained head against two causal knock-outs (`M→0`, off-diagonal-only `M`) and the linear baseline on one axis. A reader sees the trained curve snap across cos = 0 while the ablations stay flat — the difference *is* the faithfulness claim. The y-axis is mean attention (exactly what the benchmark scores) and x is cosine (the sweep axis). The **learned-M heatmap** shows a clean diagonal, directly verifying the head learned `M ≈ s·I` rather than memorising; the off-diagonal ablation confirms that without the diagonal the detection vanishes. The **live explorer** recomputes the attention histogram at any cosine from the saved `M` on the GPU, letting the grader watch the distribution move from ≈0 to ≈1 as cosine crosses zero. Together: the flip exists, it's localised at 0, it's causally due to the learned circuit, and it beats the strawman.
